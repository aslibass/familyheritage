01 — The Man

Who He
Was

Terence Joseph grew up in Panahpur, a village in Uttar Pradesh with deep roots in Indian Christianity — a community shaped by faith since the late nineteenth century. He was ordained as a Methodist minister and called to a mission that would take him to the far side of Asia.

In May 1959, the Methodist Church in Southern Asia commissioned him and his wife — a trained public health nurse — as India’s first missionary couple to Sarawak. Together they crossed to Borneo, where they would spend the next fifteen years among the Iban people of the Rajang River valley.

He ministered and he wrote. A 1964 thesis completed at Trinity Theological College, Singapore — The Long-house Church in Sarawak — surveyed the history and results of Iban Methodist mission and drew on five years of his own letters from the field. It was cited as a primary source in peer-reviewed scholarship nearly fifty years later.

From 1973 to 1974 he served as Principal of the Methodist Theological School in Sibu and Superintendent of the Sibu District — the only Indian in a succession of American principals, shaping the formation of Sarawak’s indigenous pastors.

His son Samuel — Viren Joseph’s father — made the journey to Sarawak with them as a young boy. He grew up in Borneo.

02 — The Field

The Far River

…past the last road, into longhouse country where the forest closes over the water.

The Rajang is one of the longest rivers in Southeast Asia. From its mouth near Sibu it reaches hundreds of kilometres into the Bornean interior — past Kapit, past the last road, into longhouse country where the only way forward is upriver.

The Iban had built their lives along these rivers for centuries. Christianity had been moving among them since the 1940s. By 1959 there were thousands of Iban Christians across the Rajang valley — but far too few pastors to serve them.

The answer was the tuai sembayang: Iban lay worship leaders, trained to lead their communities between the monthly visits of a minister who might travel hours by river to reach them.

He documented this system. He trained the people who ran it. And he argued — in writing, in a thesis, in the record — that the future of the Iban church lay with the Iban themselves.

A glossary of the field
People

Iban

The largest indigenous group in Sarawak — and a remarkable mass movement to Christianity from the 1940s onward.

Dwelling

The longhouse

Communal homes often 100–200 m long, housing dozens of families — the fundamental social and spiritual unit of Iban life.

Iban: “worship leader”

Tuai sembayang

Lay leaders trained to lead Sunday services between pastor visits. Courses at Nanga Mujong, Baleh River, 1966 and 1968.
